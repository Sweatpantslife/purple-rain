# PART ONE — OVERVIEW

## 1. Logline

When an ordinary city water engineer is framed as the mastermind of a vast data-falsification conspiracy, he loses his job, his home, his wife, and his name in a single week — and must descend through the criminal underworld of a rain-drowned city to discover the truth: he wasn't framed for anything he was going to do. He was framed to erase something he had already done — and the man who chose him is the man who taught him everything.

## 2. Synopsis (one page)

**Adam Vale** is a senior hydrological engineer at the Meridian Water Authority: competent, honest, quietly happy. Married to **Mara**, a pediatric nurse in the poor riverside district of Lowmarsh where he grew up. His life is small and good.

Then, over seventy-two hours, it is amputated. A dawn raid. Forty-six federal and state counts: fraud, money laundering, and — strangely specific — *falsification of hydrological field data, 2018–2020*. The prosecution's story: Adam Vale was the hub of a ring that fabricated contamination data about the city's water in order to extort developers and short municipal bonds. The evidence is overwhelming, immaculate, and entirely false. His accounts are frozen. His home is seized. His wife is shown proof of a mistress who does not exist. His brother goes silent. The plea deal on the table requires him to swear, under oath, that his own field data was a lie.

He refuses, runs, and falls — into the **Undercroft**, the flooded under-city beneath Lowmarsh, among the people the city discarded before it discarded him. There, guided by a defrocked church archivist called the **Verger**, a disgraced journalist who was destroyed by the same playbook six years earlier, and a smuggler queen who ferries the drowned tunnels, Adam learns to survive, to steal, to read the coded language of Meridian's oldest institution — a private civic cabal called **the Vestry** — and to investigate the conspiracy from beneath, where its cameras cannot see.

What he finds: the rain-borne folklore of Lowmarsh is true. The old **Halcyon Dye Works** — the mill that made the first synthetic purple dye, and then quieter, worse chemistry in two wars — has been leaking into the city's aquifer for decades, held back by a secret pump system maintained to this day. Adam's own routine 2019 tracer study proved the leak. It was buried by people he trusted. And now, on the eve of the largest privatization deal in the city's history, that buried study is the single thread that could unravel everything — so its author had to be unwritten: not killed, but *discredited*, so that everything he ever signed becomes legally worthless.

The conspiracy stays ahead of him at every turn — because one of his new friends is selling him, because the anonymous ally guiding his investigation is its architect, and because some of his bravest victories were choreographed to serve his enemies. To climb out, Adam must find the leak inside his own life, unmask a mentor, forgive a brother, bury that brother, and finally accept that in a war of documents against the people who write the documents, paper always loses.

So he stops fighting with paper. In the final act, during a hundred-year storm, on the live-broadcast day the city votes to hand its water to its poisoners, Adam makes the one witness they can never frame, bribe, or burn take the stand: **the water itself**. The concealment pumps fail, the ceremonial fountain runs violet, the flooded streets of Lowmarsh run purple downhill into the cameras — and the sky over Meridian testifies.

He does not get his old life back. He gets the truth, his name, a measured revenge, and the chance to build a new life that is his. Heaven, it turns out, is not a restoration. It is a beginning.

## 3. The World

### 3.1 Meridian

A mid-Atlantic-feeling river-delta city of about two million, built on marsh, brick, and dye money. Meridian has three vertical layers, and the game uses all of them:

- **The Heights** — Cathedral Hill and **Vestry Row**: old money, granite, private clubs, the law. Glasswater, the modern downtown of towers and lobbies, grew up in its shadow and answers to it more than it knows.
- **The Flats** — **Lowmarsh**: the riverside working district that dye built and then poisoned. Brick terraces, the shuttered **Halcyon Dye Works**, St. Brigid's church and free clinic, chronic flooding, chronic sickness, and a folklore nobody upstairs listens to. Adam's childhood home. Also the **Pier District**, a decaying port strip full of half-dead redevelopment schemes (see RH-02).
- **The Undercroft** — the drowned under-city: nineteenth-century storm drains, dye-works tunnels, sunken chapel crypts, and cellars abandoned to the water when the lower pumps were shut off in 1968. Off the map, off the grid, home to the people the city threw away. Hell — and, structurally, the one place the conspiracy is blind.

It rains in Meridian the way it snows in other stories: constantly, meaningfully, in every register from mist to biblical.

### 3.2 The purple rain

Lowmarsh folklore, generations old: *when the rain runs purple, the Works are leaking again.* Old-timers remember violet sheens on flooded curbs after big storms; children are told it's paint, or algae, or nothing. The graffiti under the Marsh Gate underpass reads **WHEN THE RAIN RUNS PURPLE** in letters repainted by unknown hands every time the city buffs it (C-02).

It is not folklore. The Works made mauveine — the world's first synthetic purple dye — and later aniline chemistry for two wars, and its unlined waste basins (**the Vats**) have been bleeding into the storm system and the aquifer for seventy years. In heavy storms, dye-marked leachate used to surface in the Flats. Since 1974 a hidden interception-pump system (**the Nightingale service**) has caught the overflow before it daylights. The purple rain didn't stop. It was *hidden*.

The title of the game is the oldest clue in it.

### 3.3 The Vestry

Meridian's true government is a private one. **The Vestry** began in 1877 as a charitable guild of mill owners meeting in the vestry room of St. Alban's chapel; it survives today as an unlisted civic club on Cathedral Hill — part board, part congregation, part conspiracy of continuity. Perhaps thirty members across the city's founding families, banks, and institutions; an inner **Quorum** of five who decide.

Two properties of the Vestry drive the game's design:

1. **It thinks in generations.** The Vestry does not commit crimes; it *maintains arrangements*. Its members genuinely believe they are the city's immune system. Its worst acts are always framed internally as stewardship.
2. **It writes everything down — in cipher.** A nineteenth-century habit that became doctrine: minutes kept in hymn-number ciphers, payments recorded as endowments, decisions memorialized in dedication plaques, stained glass, and cornerstones. *The city is the ledger.* This is why the game's riddles are diegetic: the puzzles the player solves are the Vestry's own century-old habit of hiding its business in plain, consecrated sight. (This resolves GAME_DESIGN.md's open question on riddle authorship.)

The Vestry's exiled archivist — the **Verger** — still lives, below. He is the reason the ledger can be read.

# PART TWO — THE TRUTH
### (§4. Full spoilers: the conspiracy, its mechanics, and why it targets Adam Vale)

## 4.1 What actually happened (the buried history)

The **Halcyon Dye Works** (founded 1871 by Aldous Voss) made Meridian rich on synthetic purple. During both world wars its aniline lines made feedstocks for explosives and worse; waste went into unlined basins — the Vats — dug into the marsh. The Works closed in 1968. In 1974, with violet leachate surfacing after every major storm, the Vestry's Quorum voted what its cipher-minutes call **the Quiet Covenant** (T-1974): seal the site *on paper*, curate the records, and quietly install and maintain interception pumps — the **Nightingale service** — to catch the overflow before it reaches daylight. Cheaper than remediation by three orders of magnitude. Renewable annually. Forever.

In **1988**, a gifted young engineer named **Raymond Cordell** was chosen to sign the state-mandated closure re-certification of the Vats: *contained, inert, no pathway to the aquifer*. He knew it was false — he had seen the Nightingale hall. He signed. That signature (**the First Lie**, T-1988) made his career: chief engineer of the Meridian Water Authority within a decade, the most trusted technical name in the city. Everything the Vestry ever needed certified after 1988, Cordell certified.

In **2019**, a routine post-flood integrity program run by Cordell's own best protégé — **Adam Vale** — included tracer-dye testing of the aquifer margins. Adam's study, **TS-19/44**, proved a live hydraulic connection from the Vats to the aquifer feeding Lowmarsh's intake. He reported it up, proud of the catch. Cordell buried it: the archived copy was doctored, the anomaly reattributed to sensor error, remediation logged as "completed," Adam quietly promoted out of the program (C-28). Adam believed the system had worked. He filed his field notebook and forgot.

In **2020**, the sampling tech who had run Adam's lab work, **Coby Anders**, leaked fragments to *Meridian Courier* investigative reporter **Iris Duval**. The Vestry ran what would become its signature play for the first time (T-2020): don't kill the story — *kill the author*. Duval was framed for fabricating sources, publicly destroyed, and fired. Anders drowned in an "accident" three months later. Nobody ever connected any of it. (This is the playbook's proven first run; Adam's frame is its industrialized second run.)

In **2024**, the epidemiology institute Konstantin Voss funds — publicly philanthropy, privately an early-warning system — confirmed internally what St. Brigid's nurses already suspected from their waiting rooms: the contamination reached the municipal drinking-water intake around 2014, and the Lowmarsh cancer cluster (bladder cancer, the dye-worker's disease — the one killing Konstantin Voss himself, the one that killed Adam's father) is real, measurable, and will surface in public health data within a decade. The clock the Vestry had assumed was geological turned out to be actuarial.

## 4.2 What the conspirators are REALLY after

This is the story's deepest structural twist (**R2**), and it inverts the player's mid-game assumption.

The mid-game read — *they're hiding the contamination so the big privatization deal can go through* — is wrong in the way all the best wrong answers are: it's what the evidence suggests and it's backwards.

The truth: **the deal exists because the contamination can no longer be hidden.** The Vestry's actuaries know disclosure is now inevitable — the epidemiology will surface with or without them. So the endgame, **Meridian Ascension** (the Renewal ballot measure + the federal CLEARWATER grant + the watershed transfer to a private trust), is not a concealment. It is a **controlled demolition of their own secret**:

1. **Transfer first.** Move the water system into the Ascension Trust with airtight indemnity: all pre-transfer environmental liability capped and assigned to the public side.
2. **"Discover" the contamination later** — on their schedule, through their institute, framed as a tragic legacy no living person could have known about (every record proving early knowledge having been destroyed, superseded, or *pre-discredited*).
3. **Get paid to fix it.** Kessler's trust has quietly acquired the region's three major remediation contractors (C-22). The multi-billion-dollar, decades-long, publicly funded cleanup of the Vats will be performed — at cost-plus — by the poisoners' own portfolio.

They are not hiding the poison. **They are farming it.** The conspiracy's true product is not secrecy; it is *the ownership of the moment of discovery*.

## 4.3 Why Adam Vale — specifically (the frame's real target is the past)

The public assumes — and the player is invited to assume — that Adam was a patsy of convenience, framed to carry blame *forward*. Wrong (**R1**): the frame is aimed *backward*.

The CLEARWATER grant (the deal's financial keystone) carries a non-waivable condition: **independent federal re-validation of all historical hydrological records** for any transferred system. Cordell's office controls the state-level re-certification feed — but the federal validator samples archival records directly against three registries the Vestry cannot scrub: the state environmental registry, court escrow from an old easement suit, and the utility's insurer archive. In all three sits **TS-19/44** — stamped, signed, raw-data-appended — flatly contradicting the clean record. The one thread that unspools everything, all the way back to 1988.

They cannot destroy every copy. So they destroy the *author*. Under state evidence and licensure rules, a professional engineer convicted of falsifying field data has **all** work product from the fraud period presumptively invalidated — and a **records-compromise order** (see §4.5) then authorizes wholesale certified *reconstruction* of the tainted archive. The frame converts TS-19/44 from a smoking gun into "the forged centerpiece of a convicted fraudster's extortion scheme." Every future surfacing of the true data is pre-poisoned.

Why Adam *specifically* is the perfect author to destroy — four interlocking reasons, and the fourth is the knife:

1. **He wrote the thread.** TS-19/44 is his stamp, his raw data, his chain of custody. Discredit him, void it.
2. **His biography convicts him.** Adam grew up in Lowmarsh; his father died of the cluster cancer; he had documented "grievance" against the Works and the families behind it. The prosecution's story — *he fabricated contamination data out of revenge and greed* — is credible **because it is adjacent to the truth**. The same facts that make him right make him frameable. The frame's cruel elegance: *the reason he's believable as a liar is the reason he told the truth.*
3. **He is reachable.** A brother with purchased debts (the physical-access vector, §4.4), a wife whose clinic depends on Trust-linked funding, a small life with thin legal armor.
4. **The architect knows him.** The man who designed the frame — who knew TS-19/44 existed, knew Adam kept private field notebooks (he *gave* Adam his first one), knew which dates to put in the allocution, knew Adam's habits, movements, marriage, and mind — is **Raymond Cordell** (**R3/R4**). Cordell buried the report in 2019; re-validation would expose *him first*, and behind him the First Lie of 1988 on which his entire life stands. The Vestry needed *a* solution; Cordell authored *this* one — the erasure of his own student — because it was also his own alibi. Adam wasn't framed for what he was going to do. He was framed for what Cordell had already done.

**Cordell's one miscalculation** — the error the whole endgame grows from: he assured the Quorum Adam would take the plea. Cordell judged his student by his own capitulation at the same age — *"He was offered the same door I was offered. I opened it. I assumed he would."* But Cordell himself had spent twenty years teaching Adam that *the data doesn't lie, people do* — so when the moment came, Adam trusted the data, and ran to find it.

## 4.4 The frame, mechanically (why it is airtight — and its one flaw)

Built over six months (full construction schedule in TIMELINE.md §3), triggered early when Adam, handling a routine re-validation notice, noticed the archived TS-19/44 didn't match his memory and emailed Records QA about it (C-04) — an inbox Cordell's office had already intercepted (C-09).

- **The money:** an offshore ledger seeded across 2019–2025 showing payments to Adam from a fictitious network of short-sellers and a fake "environmental consultancy," assembled by a corrupt broker (**Emil Skerry**) using backdated instruments. *The single technical flaw in the whole frame lives here:* one backdated 2023 wire uses an interbank message format that did not exist until 2024 (C-27). Found late, it is the crack that flips DA Hale.
- **The access:** Adam's brother **Theo**, gambling debts quietly purchased by an Aegis shell, was paid for "errands" — including lending out Adam's spare MWA badge (C-07), producing badge logs placing Adam at Records on falsification-relevant nights.
- **The mistress:** a two-year fabricated digital persona, "**Violet Marsh**" — a second phone, texts, hotel charges — built to detonate Adam's marriage and isolate him. The name is the frame's signature vanity: *violet* and *marsh*, the two things being buried, hidden in plain sight (C-35). Duval, later: "Whoever built her had a poet's contempt."
- **The witness:** Skerry, retained to testify that Adam recruited him. Disposable — and disposed of in Act II, with the murder pinned on Adam (RH-08).
- **The legal kill-shot:** the plea. Count 31 and the proposed allocution require Adam to swear he falsified *hydrological field data, 2018–2020* (C-11) — dates bracketing exactly TS-19/44 and exactly Cordell's supervision window. A confession is the gold standard of record-poisoning: not even a hostile future auditor can resurrect data its own author swore was false. **This is why Adam is alive.** A dead engineer's certifications *stand* — death would trigger successor review of his work. A discredited living author voids everything he ever touched. (When Adam later becomes more dangerous alive than dead — after A2-S11 — the calculus changes, over Cordell's objection, and the kill-team era begins.)
- **The bail:** posted by "Fidelis Surety" — an Aegis cutout (C-12). They *wanted* him loose once the plea stalled: flight is public confession, and a fugitive can be authored freely (new crimes attributed at will) — and used (§4.5).
- **The retroactive bonus:** framing Adam as the hub of a *ring* retroactively recasts the 2020 Duval leak as his earlier scheme — cleaning up the playbook's first run and making Duval his "accomplice or dupe." The fake conspiracy is a mirror image of the real one: **they invented a conspiracy of truth-fakers to protect a conspiracy of truth-fakers.**

## 4.5 How the antagonist stays ahead (until the final act)

Four mechanisms, each with fair-play clues and a distinct debunk/inversion beat (full table in MYSTERY_LOGIC.md §4):

1. **The rat — Felix "Nine" Novak** (double agent). The Undercroft broker who befriends Adam reports to Silas Marr, coerced via his imprisoned sister's safety. Every Aegis near-miss correlates with jobs Nine brokered (C-15, C-20, C-21). *Inverted at A3-S11:* Adam flips him into a triple agent.
2. **The hand on the board — "ROOK9"** (the hidden enemy posing as ally). The anonymous chess-app benefactor whose tips keep saving Adam is **Cordell**, steering the investigation (see 3). *Inverted at A3-S12* by a canary trap; identified by idiom, chess habit, and the taught "Cordell shortcut" (C-05, C-06, C-14, C-29).
3. **The choreography.** The conspiracy's masterstroke: some of Adam's victories are *theirs*. Rook's tip sends Adam to "rescue" the original TS-19/44 from an archive purge (A2-S07). The heist succeeds because it was staged to: skeleton crew, pre-staged box, exit-only alarm (C-17). Results: the fugitive's fingerprints taint the original's chain of custody, and — the real prize — the break-in triggers the statutory **records-compromise order** authorizing certified reconstruction of the historical archive by Cordell's office (A2-S09). The player performs the heist personally and feels the win; the reveal (A4-S02 stitch) lands as a gut-punch on their own gameplay. *Inverted structurally:* everything Adam finds via the Undercroft — the Verger, Kray's tunnels, the Nightingale hall, the Concordance — is invisible to the Vestry, because **the discarded people are the one ledger the Vestry never kept.** The theme is the mechanism.
4. **The narrative machine.** The Vestry doesn't chase Adam so much as *author* him — new crimes attributed (the warehouse fire, Skerry's murder), the "disinformation network" frame absorbing every leak (Duval's premature story, A3-S07). They own the well; everything Adam pours into it comes out poisoned. *Inverted at A4-S07:* Adam stops pouring testimony into their well and makes the well itself testify.

## 4.6 Why the villains lose (the endgame's logic)

Adam's final gambit works because it is built entirely from what the frame cannot reach:

- **Physical truth, live and public.** During the ratification ceremony — inside a broadcast ritual *the deal itself invented* (CLEARWATER requires public "first-draw certification sampling") — the storm-overwhelmed, sabotage-tipped Nightingale system fails, and dye-marked leachate daylights on camera: the fountain, the first-draw glass, the flooded streets running violet downhill into Ascension Plaza. State Water Board sampling on the spot, sealed, chain-of-custody clean, witnessed by a million phones. You cannot indict the rain.
- **Untainted provenance lattice.** Every document Adam finally fields comes from people the Vestry discarded and therefore never tracked: the exiled Verger's **Concordance** (the Vestry's own cipher-minutes, 1877–1974, plus Cordell's 1988 certificate), **Lena Voss's** private family letters, **Mara's** clinic cluster records, **Whitlock's** parallel police file, **Marr's** own insurance archive. The frame poisoned every registry it knew about. It could not poison what it did not know survived.
- **The system eats its own.** The Vestry's contingency file names Marr as the *next* designated author-of-all-crimes (**R5**, C-31). Adam mails it to him. The fixer walks off the board at the decisive moment — the conspiracy's operational arm severed by its own doctrine that *someone must always sign*.
- **The flaw in the money.** C-27 (the anachronistic wire format) gives DA Hale a personal exit — proof the case he built was fed to him — and Hale flips fast enough to matter.

Examined backwards, the villains' plan remains sound at every step *given their information*; they lose only through the two things they structurally could not price: the Undercroft's illegibility, and Cordell's misjudgment of his own student. Both are the same error. **The Vestry cannot model people it has already written off.**

# PART THREE — CHARACTERS

Format: role — motivation — arc — secret/function. Full clue relationships in MYSTERY_LOGIC.md.

## 5.1 Protagonist

### ADAM VALE, 38 — senior hydrological engineer, Meridian Water Authority
**Backstory.** Born 1988 in Lowmarsh — the year of the First Lie, though he'll never quite register the coincidence until Cordell does, bitterly, at the end. Son of **Gabriel Vale**, a pipefitter who worked demolition jobs in the old Works and died of bladder cancer in 2007, when Adam was nineteen; the disease was never connected to anything, because nothing was ever connected to anything in Lowmarsh. Adam got out the only way the Flats allowed: scholarship, engineering school, and a mentor — Raymond Cordell, who saw a marsh kid with a ferocious instinct for how water moves and made him a project. Cordell gave him his first field notebook and his creed: *"The data doesn't lie. People do. Write it down."* Adam has filled forty-one notebooks since. He married Mara at St. Brigid's — in front of a violet stained-glass window neither of them ever really looked at (C-33). He is not a hero-shaped man: he is methodical, gentle, a little rigid, allergic to shortcuts, the person colleagues call when the numbers feel wrong. His whole identity rests on one load-bearing belief: *systems work; findings get filed; the record is real.*

**Arc (heaven and hell, figuratively and structurally).**
- *Grace:* a man who trusts the record.
- *The Fall:* the record turns on him — and he learns it was never neutral; it has authors.
- *Hell:* stripped of name, stamp, and paper identity, he discovers what remains: competence, memory, and the discarded people who navigate by *word and water* instead of documents. He becomes capable — lockpicks, lies, violence — and confronts the fear that the frame was a prophecy: that he is becoming what they said he was.
- *Purgatory:* he chooses what kind of man climbs out. He forgives Theo, spares (canonically) the man who killed him, and — the true pivot — stops trying to *out-document* the documenters.
- *Reckoning:* he reclaims his name not by restoring the old record but by making truth undeniable outside all records — and then, in the epilogue, by starting a new ledger honestly. His final act is giving a rookie engineer a blank field notebook: the creed passed on, amended: *"The data doesn't lie. People do. Write it down anyway."*

**Design note (player-verb alignment):** Adam's superpower is *reading water* — flow, pressure, sediment, stain. The game's investigation and puzzle mechanics are his profession diegetically: tracer logic, valve networks, pressure maps. He solves the city like a hydraulics problem.

### MARA VALE, 36 — pediatric nurse, St. Brigid's free clinic, Lowmarsh
Warm, dry-witted, harder-working than anyone upstairs will ever know. She has spent years quietly logging what she calls "my sick kids map" — the Lowmarsh cluster, visible from a triage desk decades before any journal will print it (C-01). **Arc:** stands by Adam through the arrest until the frame's marriage-killer detonates — Violet Marsh, the offshore account with her name forged on it, and the badge-log night Adam can't explain (C-07) — and, with her clinic's funding held hostage, she signs the protective order and walks (A1-S05). The player is meant to half-believe she betrayed him (RH-03); she didn't — she was demolished, by professionals. Her climb is parallel to Adam's: she re-investigates on her own from the clinic ledgers, leaves a first signal at Theo's grave (A3-S09), gives Adam the cluster data *for the kids, not for him* (A3-S15), and nearly dies for it. **Ending:** not a reset — a beginning; she meets the new man and decides she likes him on his own merits.

### THEO VALE, 29 — Adam's brother; gig courier; gambler
The kid Lowmarsh kept. Loves Adam with the abrasive shame of the sibling who never launched. His debts were bought (T-2025e) and he was paid for "errands" he half-knew were wrong — including lending Adam's spare badge (C-07). He is the frame's unwitting hinge, and he knows it before anyone else does; the knowledge eats him alive on screen for two acts (fair-play behavioral clue). Small planted fact, huge later: **Theo faints at needles** — established as a joke in A0-S06 (C-26). **Arc:** guilt → confession under the worst circumstances (wearing Marr's wire, A2-S13) → forgiveness he can't accept → a doomed solo attempt to fix it by giving Duval his confession on tape → murdered by Aegis in a staged overdose (A3-S08) that Adam alone can read as murder, because Theo would never, ever, have used a needle. The game's low point and the revenge-temptation fulcrum.

## 5.2 Allies (the discarded)

### THE VERGER (Ezra Fenn), 71 — keeper of the Lantern shelter; exiled archivist of the Vestry
The game's Virgil. For decades he was the Vestry's own archivist-verger — keeper of the cipher-minutes — and, before that, sacristan at St. Brigid's, where in 1989 a dying Works foreman, Uriah Bell, confessed the truth of the Vats to him (T-1989). When he raised it, he was defrocked on manufactured charges (the playbook's prototype), erased, and driven below — where he did the one thing the Vestry never imagined: **he kept copying.** His hand-made duplicate of the cipher-minutes, the **Concordance**, is the only record of the Vestry that exists outside the Vestry. He runs the Lantern, the Undercroft's shelter, on three rules ("No paper. No questions. No one knows where anyone sleeps."). **Function:** riddle-mentor — he teaches Adam to read the Vestry's symbolic language, turning the puzzle pillar diegetic. **Arc:** silence → witness. His knowledge is legally worthless (hearsay from a dead man, records held by a discredited exile) — he knows it, and it has hollowed him; his arc completes when Adam builds the provenance lattice that finally lets the Concordance *count*. **Red-herring duty:** he bears the Vestry's mark (a brand-scar) and knows far too much; the player is invited to distrust him (RH-04) before learning the mark is exile, not membership.

### IRIS DUVAL, 55 — disgraced investigative journalist; the precedent
Once the *Meridian Courier*'s finest; destroyed in 2020 by the playbook's first run for touching a leak she never knew came from Adam's own study. Six years a "conspiracy crank" with a crazy wall that is 60% noise and 40% dead accurate — including Coby Anders' drowning and the phrase that names the game's method: *"They don't kill stories. They kill authors."* (C-16). **Motivation:** vindication — which is her flaw. **Wildcard beat:** in Act III she publishes early, against the plan, with a document Nine seeded into Adam's stash (C-25), and hands the Vestry its third well-poisoning triumph (A3-S07). **Arc:** vindication-hunger → the discipline of going *second* — her redemption is holding the real story until after the water testifies (A4-S07), the hardest thing a burned journalist can do. Also the team's forensic-finance eye: she finds the frame's one flaw, C-27.

### ODILE KRAY, "the Ferrywoman," 49 — smuggler queen of the flooded tunnels
Runs passage, freight, and favors through the Undercroft by boat. Transactional, unsentimental, never lies, never volunteers. Sells stale routes to Aegis with the same face she sells true ones to Adam. Her map of the tunnels has one blank quarter: *"No one ferries to the Vats. Toll's too high."* (A2-S04). **Wildcard logic:** she helps no cause, only balances; she enters the endgame personally only after Marr burns one of her barges in the false-safehouse raid (A3-S11) — the Vestry finally created a debt, and Kray always collects. **Function:** access — she is how the finale reaches the Nightingale hall. Her warning "the rat swims close to the boat" is the earliest Nine clue (C-15).

### DET. SGT. DANA WHITLOCK, 44 — Major Crimes, seconded to the fraud task force
The Gerard figure: the honest hunter. Starts the game trying to catch Adam and never stops being annoyed that the case is *too good* — evidence that anticipates questions, a bail bond from a surety nobody's heard of (C-12), a document reviewed by a man who was dead when he initialed it (C-18). **Arc:** hunter → skeptic (Hale blocks her follow-ups on record — obstruction she files away) → quiet parallel investigator → the inside ally who compels the State Water Board's presence at the ceremony and delivers the arrests. She and Adam never fully trust each other and never need to; their relationship is conducted in evidence, at arm's length, like correspondence chess. **Line:** "I don't believe you. I believe your paperwork's wrong, and that's worse."

### LENA VOSS, 33 — forensic hydrologist; Konstantin's estranged granddaughter
Adam's dark mirror born on the other hill: fled the family name, works contamination cases in another state, came home only for the funerals. Her father (Konstantin's son) died estranged and drunk, having guessed. She enters late (A3-S14) — one true detail in Duval's burned story, the word *nightingale*, matched a 1974 letter in her grandfather's papers (C-30) — and brings the two things no one can taint: family letters with clean provenance, and the Voss name spoken *against* the Vestry on camera. **Motivation:** to lance the family sin rather than settle it. **Ending:** turns the Voss trust into the remediation fund — the dye money finally paying its bill.

## 5.3 The double agents

### FELIX "NINE" NOVAK, 44 — forger, broker, friend; Marr's informant (the bad-side mole)
The best document man below, charming, generous, genuinely fond of Adam — and reporting every brokered job to Silas Marr, who holds the safety of Nine's imprisoned sister over him. He is why Aegis is always ten minutes behind (C-21), and he is the hand that seeds the forged memo into Adam's stash (C-25). Fair-play texture: his fortunes tick upward after every near-miss (C-20); he asks one question too many about where Adam sleeps; he is never, ever raided. **Arc:** coerced betrayer → caught (A3-S11) → flipped into a triple agent → chooses, at the end, to make his last forgery for Adam (the contingency-file extraction, A4-S03) knowing what it costs. He survives — barely — and the sister is freed in the fallout; mercy the player may or may not feel he earned.

### "ROOK9" / RAYMOND CORDELL — the ally in the machine (the deep double agent)
The anonymous benefactor whose chess-app tips (C-14) save Adam's freedom in Act I and hand him his biggest early win — is the architect of the frame, steering the investigation to finish the conspiracy's work (§4.5). Cordell plays both mentor and minotaur; see his full entry below. The double-agent reveal is the mystery's emotional core.

## 5.4 The conspirators (the Vestry's Quorum and its instruments)

### RAYMOND CORDELL, 66 — former MWA chief engineer; Chief Certification Officer, Meridian Renewal — THE ARCHITECT
The mentor. Meridian's most trusted technical mind — a reputation built entirely on the First Lie of 1988 (T-1988), which he has spent his life protecting the way other men protect children. Not greedy; worse — *invested*: every bridge, main, and reservoir he honestly built since stands, in his mind, as justification for the one false page it all rests on. ("I signed one paper. Everything after was just maintenance.") When TS-19/44 crossed his desk in 2019 he buried it; when re-validation made the burial insufficient he designed the frame, chose his own student, and briefed the Quorum that Adam would take the plea. **He is ROOK9** — his steering of fugitive-Adam is equal parts operational necessity, chess vanity, and something he would never name: guilt looking for a way to lose. **Arc:** unseen hand → warm false ally → unmasked (A3-S12) → the cabin game where both men know and neither says (A3-S13) → final confrontation in the flooding seal chamber where it all began (A4-S08). **Key relationships:** taught Adam the notebook habit (which he then weaponized in the search warrant, C-05/C-10), the "Cordell shortcut" (which fingerprints his forgery, C-06), and the creed that ultimately defeats him. **His error:** §4.3 — he assumed his student would open the same door he did.

### VIVIAN KESSLER, 58 — chair, Meridian Trust — THE ENGINE
The pragmatist who will inherit the machine. As a young attorney in 1998 she built the shell dissolution of Voss Chemical and the liability escrow (T-1998); as Trust chair she designed the liability inversion (§4.2) — the deal, the indemnity annex, the quiet acquisition of the remediation contractors (C-22). No cruelty, no ideology; she regards the Vestry's liturgical theatrics as legacy branding and its crimes as *risk management with history attached*. She is the mid-game's hidden final boss reveal-by-attrition: when Voss dies (RH-05), nothing slows down, and the player understands the engine was never the old man. **Arc:** untouchable → visible (the gala scene, A3-S03, where she and Adam speak once, politely, unforgettably) → cornered by the provenance lattice → arrested at the airfield, immaculate and unrepentant (A4-S09): "You've mistaken me for the author. I'm only the accountant." **Design note:** she never once lies to Adam. Every sentence she speaks in the game is technically true — the mark of someone who learned early that documents, not statements, are what convict.

### KONSTANTIN VOSS, 81 — last of the dye dynasty — THE PENITENT (apparent mastermind; actually the past trying to die quietly)
Heir to the Works, dying of the dye-worker's cancer his family gave Lowmarsh (C-32). Funded the epidemiology institute as philanthropy-shaped reconnaissance (§4.1). For two acts the evidence points to him as the spider (RH-05) — old money, old sin, motive of legacy — and he *is* guilty, of everything, for decades; but he is the machine's conscience, not its will. **Wildcard beat:** the dying man summons Adam in secret (A3-S05), half-confesses in the Vestry's own riddle-idiom ("ask the window what the rain paid for," C-23), gives him the watch-fob key to the family archive — and is dead within days, "peacefully, in his sleep," with the ambiguity left deliberately unresolved (the player may suspect Kessler ordered it; no proof is ever offered; the Vestry's mercy and its murder look identical, which is the point). **Function:** the reveal that decapitating a system changes nothing — Act IV begins with the head cut off and the machine accelerating.

### SILAS MARR, 52 — director, Aegis Meridian — THE HUNTER
Ex-military intelligence; ran the 2020 Duval operation (his professional masterpiece until now); runs the hunt for Adam, the Nightingale maintenance cover, and every wet deletion (Anders, Skerry, Theo). Not a sadist — a *maintainer*: his creed, delivered graveside (A3-S09), is "Order isn't clean. It's maintained." He is the antagonist the player runs from for three acts, the visible avatar of the machine — which makes his ending the story's coldest irony: the Vestry's contingency file (**R5**, C-31) designates *him* the next author-of-all-crimes, the fall guy for the entire arrangement should it ever surface. Adam mails him his own future. **Arc:** hunter → obsessive → *reader* — the one man in the Vestry's employ professional enough to believe evidence when it's about himself — → walks off the board at the Nightingale standoff (A4-S06), taking his insurance archive with him, leaving his employers naked. Canonically he faces trial with a proffer; a player choice can instead end him in the pump hall. He never apologizes for Theo. Some doors stay shut.

### MARCUS HALE, 47 — District Attorney — THE INSTRUMENT
The prosecution's public face; gubernatorial ambitions; believes perhaps 70% of his own case and suppresses the other 30% with careerist discipline (his on-record blocking of Whitlock's follow-ups becomes evidence against him, A3-S02). Not Vestry — *used* by the Vestry, which is the reveal that breaks him: when Duval's C-27 (the impossible wire) proves the case was manufactured and fed to him, Hale flips with the speed of pure self-preservation (A4-S09), and his testimony helps convict Kessler. His career ends anyway. Nobody mourns. RH-07 invites the player to read him as inner circle; he never was; the banality is the point.

### Minor named: MAYOR PORTIA DEANE (the smiling face who drinks the violet first-draw on camera, A4-S07 — complicit-adjacent, never indicted, votes with whoever is winning); LT. DANE KREBS (Marr's cleanup lead; Theo's killer; the object of Adam's Act III revenge choice, A3-S10); EMIL SKERRY (the frame's rented witness, RH-08, murdered in A2-S12 and pinned on Adam); DR. AMARA CHEN (State Water Board hydrologist — the incorruptible sampler at the ceremony); HAROLD GRADY (dead MWA reviewer whose initials on the doctored report post-date his death — the forgery's fatal anachronism, C-18); COBY ANDERS (Adam's 2019 sampling tech; leaked to Duval in 2020; drowned for it — the story's first ghost); URIAH BELL (the foreman whose 1989 deathbed confession gave the Verger the truth and cost him everything).

# PART FOUR — THE FIVE ACTS
### Scriptment: scene-level beats

> Estimated pacing: A0 ≈ 45–60 min · A1 ≈ 2 h · A2 ≈ 4 h · A3 ≈ 4 h · A4 ≈ 3 h (core path).
> Clue plants are tagged (C-##), red herrings (RH-##), reveals (R#). Full cross-reference in MYSTERY_LOGIC.md.

## ACT 0 — GRACE
*The life he had. Playable prologue; tutorializes movement, interaction, and the water-reading mechanic. Tone: warm, ordinary, rain as comfort. Everything in this act is a loaded gun.*

**A0-S01 — "Anniversary Morning."**
The Vale rowhouse, Lowmarsh's nicer edge, 6:40 AM, soft rain. Tutorial: movement, interaction, the notebook (Adam jots everything; the player learns the journal UI as *his habit*). Mara, getting ready for a clinic shift, banters about tonight's tenth-anniversary dinner; offhand, tired: "Three more kids on the ward this month from the Flats. Swear that neighborhood is cursed" (**C-01**). Radio: the Meridian Renewal ballot measure, "Ascension" ad copy — *A City Reborn*. The player leaves the house holding everything Adam is about to lose.

**A0-S02 — "The City in the Rain."**
Walk-and-tram traversal to work; establishes Meridian's three layers visually. Ascension billboards over Lowmarsh scaffolding; a protest sticker (WHOSE WATER?); Vestry Row's granite silence glimpsed from the tram. In the Marsh Gate underpass, the repainted graffiti: **WHEN THE RAIN RUNS PURPLE** (**C-02**). An old man feeding pigeons tells the "purple rain" folktale unprompted if engaged — flagged by the player as color, filed by the game as thesis.

**A0-S03 — "The Waterworks."**
MWA offices. Competence-tutorial: a live low-pressure anomaly in Sector 9 — Adam diagnoses it elegantly via the flow-map interface (teaches the tracer-logic puzzle grammar). Colleagues establish his standing; his P.E. stamp is shown, casually, in use (the object the whole plot orbits). In his inbox: a CLEARWATER-grant memo — *independent re-validation of historical hydrological records to commence, all engineers to confirm archival integrity of prior filings* (**C-03**). Mundane. Fatal.

**A0-S04 — "The File."**
Adam, dutiful, pulls his old filings — including **TS-19/44**, the 2019 tracer study. Interactive discrepancy-spot (the mechanic's first real use): Figure 12 in the archive doesn't match his memory; he checks field notebook #29 against it — the archived curve is *wrong*, cleaner, sensor-error-shaped (**C-04**). He assumes a scanning mix-up and sends a one-line email to Records QA: "Archived TS-19/44 fig. 12 doesn't match field data — wrong version scanned? Happy to re-submit. — AV." Send. **This is the tripwire.** The player will not know for ten hours of gameplay that this click ended Adam's life.

**A0-S05 — "Chess with Ray."**
Lunch, rooftop cafeteria, chessboard worn smooth. Raymond Cordell: warm, wry, retirement-party jokes. He gifts Adam a new field notebook — "Forty-two. The data doesn't lie, people do. Write it down." (**C-05**). Mentor-lore drops naturally: "castle early, castle often"; the famous **Cordell shortcut** for aquifer-boundary math (**C-06**). Cordell asks, idly, what Adam's working on; Adam mentions the odd archive scan of the old tracer study. A beat of nothing on Cordell's face — the most expensive nothing in the game. (Replay/NG+: this is the scene players will rewatch.)

**A0-S06 — "Theo."**
Theo intercepts Adam outside the office — too bright, too funny, new phone, asking for a "bridge" loan. Adam gives him less than he asks, more than he should. Environmental storytelling if the player looks: betting slips in Theo's jacket, a second phone; and later that evening at home, Adam's desk drawer — **the spare MWA badge isn't in it** (**C-07**). A needle-drop of foreshadowing played as a joke: Theo, re: Mara's flu-shot nagging — "You know me and needles. I'd rather have the flu" (**C-26**).

**A0-S07 — "The Anniversary."**
Dinner in Lowmarsh, across from St. Brigid's — where they married. Through the restaurant window, the church's violet-tinted glass (**C-33** — the window depicts St. Brigid holding back a violet flood; visible now, comprehensible only in Act III). Warm scene, real marriage, real jokes; the toast: "Ten years. Nothing about us in the papers. May it stay boring." Outside, rain thickening; a stranger under an awning takes their photograph and does not lower the phone (**C-08** — Aegis pre-operation surveillance, noticeable only to attentive players).

**A0-S08 — "Purple Dawn."**
Three days later. 5:50 AM, hard rain. Adam, sleepless-ish, checks email: his QA note has bounced — *recipient not found* (**C-09** — the intercepted inbox). Doorbell. Then the door. **The raid** — played interactively and helplessly: hands on the wall, the house strip-searched, Mara's face across the room, evidence bags — and the warrant, glimpsed for two seconds, listing with impossible specificity *"personal field notebooks (approx. 41 vols.)"* (**C-10** — only someone who knew the habit could have written that line). Notebooks seized — all but #42, Cordell's gift, still in the car. Perp walk in the rain, flashbulbs strobing the wet street violet-white. **SMASH TITLE: PURPLE RAIN.**

## ACT I — THE FALL
*Everything stripped away in real time. The player learns the fugitive loop: investigate, act, puzzle, consequence. Tone: vertigo. The system is not malfunctioning; it is functioning — for someone else.*

**A1-S01 — "Intake."**
Booking, remand. The charges read aloud — 46 counts; the player can examine the charge sheet (document-reading tutorial): buried in the RICO thicket, **Count 31: falsification of hydrological field data, 2018–2020** (**C-11** — why would a *fraud* case care so precisely about field data dates?). Dayroom TV: DA Marcus Hale's press conference — "the author of the largest environmental-data fraud in state history." *Author.* The word does a lot of work in this story.

**A1-S02 — "The Offer."**
Proffer room. Hale, flanked by a federal liaison, slides the plea: 30 months, minimum security, in exchange for allocution — sworn confession to falsifying TS-19/44 *and associated field data* — plus named accomplices ("we can help you remember names"). Adam, still believing this is a fixable clerical catastrophe: "The data is real. Test the water." Hale, without blinking: "We did. It's clean." (True — the intake tests clean *because Nightingale is running*; the frame is nested in the cover-up.) Adam refuses. His overwhelmed public defender's aside, walking out: "I've never seen a case build itself like this" (**C-13**).

**A1-S03 — "Anonymous Grace."**
Bail — posted by a surety Adam's lawyer has never heard of, **Fidelis Surety** (**C-12**). Release into a media scrum played first-person: microphones as weapons, his own face on every screen. Whitlock watches from a car, annoyed already: defendants like this don't make bail; who *wants* him out?

**A1-S04 — "House of Glass."**
Home, under siege — news vans, neighbors' eyes. Interactive domestic collapse: the termination call from MWA (license suspended pending); accounts frozen mid-grocery-order (civil forfeiture); the mortgage called — the note held, the letterhead says, by **Meridian Trust**. On TV, the evidence drip: the offshore ledger graphic, and "sources describe a longtime mistress, 'Violet M.'" Mara holds through all of it — jaw set, loyal, terrifyingly quiet.

**A1-S05 — "Everything, In Order."**
The frame detonates its marriage charge. Mara, at the kitchen table, three documents lined up like a firing squad: the second phone "found" in the garage with two years of texts to *Violet Marsh*; an offshore account bearing *her* forged signature; and the badge log placing Adam at Records on the night of their cabin weekend — the one night he has no witness but her, and she was asleep (**C-07** payoff #1). She asks one question: "Why was your badge at Records on the 14th?" He has no answer. (Theo does.) Her clinic's funding is "under review" unless she cooperates; the protective order is drafted *for* her by a helpful advisor she'll never see again (RH-03 planted: to Adam — and the player — her departure is indistinguishable from betrayal). She leaves. The house is empty and the rain is very loud.

**A1-S06 — "Theo's Silence."**
Adam finds Theo at the betting café. Confrontation: Theo lies badly, over-laughs, presses cash on him ("for the lawyers"), cries in the bathroom with the taps running. Player-findable: fresh betting slips *paid off*, a burner phone with one saved number. Adam can't make himself see it. The player can.

**A1-S07 — "The Second Wave."**
New "evidence" surfaces: a broker, **Emil Skerry**, on every channel, claiming Adam recruited him to shop fabricated contamination data to short-sellers. Re-arrest warrant imminent — bail revoked. And then the game's strangest grace: Adam's dormant chess app pings. A move played in a two-year-dead correspondence game, from a new account — **ROOK9** — with a note in the annotation field: *"They come at 6 AM. Marsh Gate is unwatched from 4:40. Take nothing with a battery. Keep the notebook habit."* (**C-14** — the corpus begins; note the idiom, the specificity, the *care*.) Adam burns an hour deciding whether it's a trap. The player decides with him.

**A1-S08 — "The Run."**
5:55 AM. The raid comes early — first true evasion setpiece: out the back, through yards and alleys of a waking Lowmarsh, storm swelling; drones, spotlights, the river rising. He goes down the Marsh Gate steps — under the WHEN THE RAIN RUNS PURPLE graffiti — into the storm tunnels, and the water takes him: a near-drowning played to blackout, ended by a grate, a boat-hook, and a woman's voice: "Toll's a story. Start talking."

**A1-S09 — "Below."**
The Undercroft. Kray's boat through drowned streets — a sunken chapel, cellar-windows glowing underwater. She deposits him at **the Lantern**: door riddle (first diegetic riddle, simple, teachable — the Verger's filter for cops and fools). Inside: heat, soup, no questions. The Verger's three rules: *"No paper. No questions. No one knows where anyone sleeps."* Adam, stripped of everything document-shaped, writes his name in notebook #42 just to see it. Above, through a storm grate, his own wanted bulletin echoes off the wet brick. **End of Act I.**

## ACT II — HELL
*Rock bottom: the discarded people, the darkest jobs, the deepest layer. Adam becomes capable — and risks becoming what they said he was. Contains the story's overall midpoint (A2-S07/S08). Tone: chthonic, strangely warm at the core; hell has better people than the surface.*

**A2-S01 — "The Rules of Below."**
Lantern orientation; the Undercroft's economy of favors. Introductions: **Nine** — forger, fixer, instantly likable ("Papers make the man, friend. Luckily I make papers") — and the Verger, who studies Adam a long moment too long. On the shelter wall, half-hidden: a brand-scar symbol Adam will later learn is **the Vestry's mark** (RH-04 planted — the player's first reason to distrust the one man who deserves trust). Quest grammar established: everything below costs a favor; every favor is a mission.

**A2-S02 — "First Job — The Pharmacy Run."**
Crime of necessity #1: the Lantern's insulin and antibiotics come from "shrinkage" at a distribution depot — this month's contact got caught, so Adam goes. Stealth-infiltration tutorial; the depot's security contractor is **Aegis Meridian** (first sight of the logo that will chase him for the whole game — even charity leaks are their business). He crosses the line — picks the lock, takes the crate — and the worst part is how *good* he is at it, and how right it feels. First tone-setting moral note: hell's crimes feed people.

**A2-S03 — "The Name Trade."**
Nine's price for a clean identity: not money — *competence*. The Undercroft's gravity-fed water feed is fouling; fix it. A true hydraulics puzzle (valve network, contamination isolation, the game's core puzzle grammar at full depth) that walks Adam — deliberately, this is the design — through the deep tunnels where he sees it with his own eyes: **violet leachate, live, sheeting down a Works-era wall** (**R0 begins**; **C-02** payoff #1). The folklore is running in real time. Old habit: he takes a sample, labels it, logs it in #42. The player feels the story's floor shift: this was never just about him.

**A2-S04 — "The Ferrywoman's Price."**
Passage negotiation with Kray for the districts Adam needs. Her tunnel map is magnificent and has one blank quarter: *"No one ferries to the Vats. Toll's too high."* Her only free gift, unprompted, watching Nine's lantern recede across the water: **"Rat swims close to the boat, engineer."** (**C-15**.) She won't elaborate. Wildcards don't.

**A2-S05 — "Iris."**
The Verger keeps old bylines; one name recurs on the true stories nobody believed: Duval. Adam finds her in a locked-down flat above a shuttered print shop — paranoid, brilliant, half-feral. Her crazy wall: red string, and in the middle of the noise, signal — the 2020 spiked story, a photo of **Coby Anders**, drowned; the phrase she's written and circled: *"They don't kill stories. They kill authors"* (**C-16**). And then the scene turns: her surviving fragment of the 2020 leak is a data table Adam *recognizes* — his own numbers, his own study. The leak that destroyed her came from TS-19/44. Guilt bomb; alliance forged in shared shrapnel. She teaches him the playbook's shape: he is its second edition.

**A2-S06 — "Rook's Second Gift."**
ROOK9 plays another move. Annotation: *County Archive Annex, Bay 9. Purge order Friday. TS-19/44 original, microfiche, custody logs, the notebooks they seized — all scheduled for the shredder. After Friday you were never right.* The mission designs itself. (The player should feel gratitude toward Rook here. That's the trap working.)

**A2-S07 — "The Archive Heist." — MIDPOINT SETPIECE**
Night infiltration of the Archive Annex, planned with Nine's floor plans and Kray's exfil. It goes *almost* suspiciously well, and every oddity is visible to a careful player and explicable to a trusting one (**C-17**): a skeleton guard roster on a purge week; Bay 9's box already staged near the loading dock; its evidence seal *already broken*; the alarm that only trips on exit — driving a thrilling, survivable boat-chase escape. Adam comes away holding the original TS-19/44, raw data, custody fiche — and the raid's seized field notebooks, #29 among them (the state's own evidence, boxed for the shredder with the rest of the case archive). First real victory. The music says so. The music is lying (see A2-S09, A4-S02).

**A2-S08 — "Reading the Water." — REVEAL R1**
The Lantern, small hours. Original vs. doctored archive copy vs. notebook #29 vs. the fresh tunnel sample: the truth assembles on screen (evidence-board mechanic debut). The doctored Figure 12 uses the **Cordell shortcut** in its fabricated math (**C-06** payoff #1 — noted now as "whoever forged this was MWA-trained; that's the shortcut Ray teaches"). The revision block carries reviewer initials **H.G. — Harold Grady — dated fourteen months after Grady died** (**C-18**; momentarily floated as "so Grady's the forger?" — RH-06, debunked in the same scene by his obituary). And the frame's geometry snaps into focus: the allocution dates (C-11), the CLEARWATER re-validation (C-03), the doctored archive (C-04) — all of it points backward. **"They didn't frame me to blame me. They framed me to unwrite me."** (**R1**.) The question mutates: not *why me* — *what did I sign?* He knows what he signed. Now he knows someone needs it unsigned.

**A2-S09 — "Heat."**
Consequence lands with both hands. News: *Fugitive engineer raids public archive; records integrity compromised* — and the state's response: an emergency **records-compromise order** authorizing "certified reconstruction of affected archives" by the Renewal's certification office. Cordell's office. Adam watches his win become their permission slip in real time (partial comprehension now; full horror at A4-S02). And the Undercroft turns dangerous: an Aegis snatch squad hits a Lantern supply drop *Adam was scheduled for* — first hard proof of a leak. Suspicion montage, deliberately mis-aimed: Duval's recklessness? Kray's transactionality? (RH-01 planted.) It was Nine (C-21 pattern begins: the player can already correlate — the near-misses touch only Nine-brokered movements).

**A2-S10 — "The Vestry Surfaces."**
The Verger sees the TS-19/44 original and the violet sample on Adam's board and stops pretending not to know things. His story, told in the dark of the drowned chapel of **St. Alban's**: the 1989 deathbed confession of Uriah Bell; his own exile; the brand on his arm — *given*, not worn (RH-04 debunked). And the education begins: the Vestry, the Quiet Covenant, the cipher habit — *"the city is the ledger; learn to read it."* First true Vestry puzzle: the drowned chapel's hymn board (numbers frozen since 1968) keys a dedication-plaque cipher yielding a 1974 covenant fragment — and a word: **the Nightingale service** (C-19 thread opens). RH-02 debunked en route: the Pier District papers Adam lifted earlier point at a decoy — the water permits don't touch the Pier; everything drains toward the Vats.

**A2-S11 — "Nightingale." — REVEAL R0 COMPLETES**
With Kray paid in future favors (she still won't ferry the last quarter-mile; he wades it), Adam reaches the edge of the Vats and finds the impossible: a **cathedral-sized Victorian pump hall, running** — maintained machinery, greased rails, violet slick in the sump, fresh bootprints, a clipboard. The conspiracy is not a cover-up; it is an *operating utility with a maintenance schedule*. He photographs everything and lifts the maintenance log: contractor of record, **Larkspur Facility Services** (**C-19**). Horror-adjacent setpiece: he is nearly caught by the night crew — hiding inside the machine that hides the crime.

**A2-S12 — "What They Say I Am."**
The Vestry escalates (this is the moment, per §4.4, the kill decision is made over Cordell's objection — visible to the player only as a change in weather: Aegis stops trying to *catch* him). Broker Emil Skerry is found dead; the murder is authored onto Adam within a news cycle (RH-08's true function: the witness was always disposable, his death a twofer). A records warehouse burns; also Adam. Bounty posted *below* — even Undercroft eyes change. Low point of the act: Adam sees his own face on a dayroom-style TV again, now over the word KILLER, and catches himself, in a wet black window, checking whether the face fits.

**A2-S13 — "The Choice in the Dark."**
Kray, evenhanded as gravity, makes the real offer: out. Tonight. Clean papers, coastal boat, a life — the option that ends the game as a tragedy the world never learns to name. (Player choice: refusing defines Adam; the design flags this as a formal "commitment beat.") And then Marr's trap springs: a meet brokered "from Theo" — and it *is* Theo, wired, terrified, weeping, Aegis in the walls. Chaos setpiece with an inviolable design rule: the game gives no mechanism to harm Theo. In the collapse and floodwater escape, Theo's confession spills — the debts, the errands, **the badge** (**C-07** payoff #2) — "I didn't know it was for *you*, Adam, I swear, I didn't *ask*—" Because he couldn't afford to. Lowmarsh's oldest skill. Adam gets him out through the water and — canonical path — forgives him, which is worse than rage: Theo now owes a debt no one can carry (fires the A3-S08 tragedy).

**A2-S14 — "Act Turn — Out of Hell."**
Regrouping at the Lantern. Adam does something new with the H.G. anachronism (C-18): *doesn't* spend it, doesn't publish it — he photographs it, keeps the original sealed and untouched, and mails the print anonymously to the one hunter whose paperwork-allergy he's clocked from news conferences: **Det. Dana Whitlock**. (Learning: provenance is ammunition; fugitives can't fire it, but cops can.) The Verger's benediction over the evidence board: *"You've mapped hell. Now climb with the map."* **End of Act II.**

## ACT III — PURGATORY
*The climb. Alliances built, reversals suffered, the leak found, the Rook unmasked. The act where Adam stops being prey. Tone: momentum with a limp; every gain pays a toll. Contains the story's LOW POINT (A3-S08) and the great REVERSAL (A3-S07).*

**A3-S01 — "The Ascent Plan."**
War-room scene at the Lantern — the game's quest map goes diegetic: cork, string, stolen fiche. Targets: the deal's paper spine (escrow, indemnity, grant conditions) and the leak in Adam's own life. Duval takes finance; the Verger takes the ledger-city; Adam takes the Trust.

**A3-S02 — "Whitlock Bites."**
(Interleaved perspective — witnessed via Duval's courthouse source and news fragments; the design keeps Adam player-locked.) Whitlock quietly verifies the H.G. anachronism — the state's own document, forged after a dead man's signature — and pulls the Fidelis Surety thread (C-12) one shell deep toward Aegis before **Hale kills her requests in writing** (obstruction, on the record — a C that convicts him of usefulness later, A4-S09). She goes quiet. Quiet, in Whitlock, is a weapon being loaded.

**A3-S03 — "The Trust Job." — SETPIECE**
The Meridian Trust gala — Renewal's victory-lap fundraiser. Social-stealth infiltration (forged invitation by Nine; suit by Kray's rag trade; face hidden in plain sight among people who only ever looked at his mugshot). Two prizes from Kessler's private floor: the **indemnity annex** (all pre-transfer environmental liability capped and publicly assigned) and the **escrow schedule** — including subsidiary acquisitions: three remediation contractors, and a line item, absurd until it isn't: *"fountain & waterscape maintenance — $3.1M/yr — Larkspur Facility Services"* (**C-22**, **C-34** — Nightingale's budget line hiding as landscaping). And the scene the whole act is remembered for: Kessler catches him — *recognizes* him — at the champagne table, and instead of calling security, talks. Two minutes, everything true, nothing helpful: "You're looking for the man who decided. Keep looking. There hasn't been one since before I was born." She lets him leave (her calculus: a panicked fugitive arrested at her gala is a headline; a doomed one is a Tuesday). Aegis arrives *fast* anyway — too fast (the leak-clock ticks: this job was Nine-brokered, C-21). Rook had warned, unusually: *"Gala's too hot. Abort."* Adam disobeyed — and now must wonder whether Rook was protecting him or the Trust (**C-29** — the ambiguity is the clue).

**A3-S04 — "The Meaning of Money." — REVEAL R2**
Duval decodes the haul, and the mid-game's premise inverts: the indemnity assigns the poison's cost to the public *before* discovery; the trust owns the cleanup contractors; the institute owns the discovery clock. **"They're not hiding the poison, Adam. They're farming it."** (**R2**.) The deal is not endangered by the truth coming out. The deal is a machine *for* the truth coming out — on their schedule, into their invoices. Despair beat: exposure, the entire strategy so far, may be exactly what they're pricing in.

**A3-S05 — "Voss's Summons."**
Through the Verger's oldest channel — a hymn-board number changed at a chapel nobody attends — the dying patriarch asks for the fugitive. Midnight, the Voss mausoleum-house on Cathedral Hill, oxygen hiss, a nurse paid to be deaf. Konstantin Voss, bladder cancer (C-32 visible: the dye's own disease), wants... absolution? A witness? He speaks only in the Vestry's idiom — half-confession, half-riddle: *"Ask the window what the rain paid for."* (**C-23**) — and presses a watch-fob key into Adam's hand ("the family's memory is in the family's keeping; my granddaughter has the keeping of it"). Aegis headlights; Adam gone through the garden. **Four days later Voss is dead** — "peacefully, in his sleep," ruled natural, timing immaculate. The game never resolves whether Kessler ordered it (deliberate ambiguity; the Vestry's mercy and its murder are indistinguishable, which *is* the reveal). RH-05 begins its collapse: the player has now met the "mastermind" and found a frightened old man.

**A3-S06 — "The Window."**
St. Brigid's, by night, with the Verger. The violet window — *St. Brigid holding back a flood* — read properly at last: the 1975 "restoration" donor panel is a payment cipher; the lancet inscriptions key to Concordance folios; the window is a *receipt*, in glass, for the Quiet Covenant, installed in the one building Lowmarsh would never tear down (**C-24**, C-33 payoff). Continuity of knowledge, 1974 onward, provable — if anything the Verger holds could ever be believed. And the private gut-punch, free of charge: framed on the sacristy wall, a wedding photo the parish keeps of local couples. Adam and Mara, ten years younger, laughing — the violet flood rising in glass behind them the entire time.

**A3-S07 — "Duval's Gun Jumps." — THE REVERSAL**
Against the plan — her byline hunger, her six starved years — Iris publishes: *THE VATS ARE LEAKING*, with the rescued TS-19/44 as centerpiece... and, unknowingly, a seeded "smoking-gun" Vestry memo that Nine slipped into Adam's document stash on Marr's orders weeks ago (**C-25** — flagged for the player, in retrospect, by being the only document on the board Adam never sourced firsthand; Duval: "it was too good; God, it was too good"). The Vestry's response is 48 hours of surgical grace: the memo exposed as a forgery (it is one), carbon-dated, mocked; therefore *everything* published presumed forged; TS-19/44 now publicly welded to a proven fake; "the fugitive's disinformation network" becomes the story of record. Third demonstration of the well-poisoning playbook (Duval 2020; the records-compromise order; now this) — the player finally sees the *pattern as the villain*. All documentary leverage: ash. Duval, on the floor of her flat, surrounded by her life's second ruin: "I handed them the match."

**A3-S08 — "Theo." — THE LOW POINT**
Theo, carrying his unpayable debt (A2-S13), tries to fix it alone: approaches the disgraced Duval with the one thing that could resurrect her story — his own taped confession about the badge. Marr's cleanup is faster. **Theo dies in a supervised-looking squat, staged overdose, needle in his arm.** The city shrugs: junkie brother of a fraud. Adam knows in one breath it's murder — *Theo fainted at needles* (**C-26** payoff; the A0-S06 joke detonates six acts of sorrow) — and knows in the second breath that forgiving Theo is what killed him: absolution sent him looking for penance. The game's darkest hour. The rain does not care.

**A3-S09 — "The Funeral."**
Theo's burial, minimal, wet. Adam attends disguised inside a gravedigger's crew — and Marr *lets him*, which is the scene: the hunter appears beside him at the treeline, gun holstered, voice even, and delivers the antagonist thesis graveside: *"Order isn't clean, Mr. Vale. It's maintained. You maintained water; I maintain arrangements. You'd have understood each other, our jobs."* He offers the last courtesy — leave tonight, the city forgets you in a year — and walks away, confident as weather (the villain's peak; he is never more ahead than now, and never again). Across the grave: **Mara** — who does not look at Adam, and leaves her clinic pin on Theo's headstone. It is not for Theo (Theo teased her about that pin; she'd never part with it idly). It is a signal flare with a pin backing: *I've started counting my dead differently.* (RH-03 begins its debunk.)

**A3-S10 — "Rage." — PLAYER-CHOICE VALVE**
Nine's channels cough up the cleanup detail: **Lt. Dane Krebs**, Aegis, handled Theo. Adam hunts him through a rain-flooded transit yard — corners him — and the game opens its formal choice valve (per GAME_DESIGN.md's open question, tracked but not moralized): **kill him / flip him / walk away**. Canonical: Adam, at the edge, hears his own creed come back in Cordell's voice — *the data doesn't lie* — and takes evidence over blood: Krebs's phone, geotagged Aegis field notes. The pattern is now undeniable (**C-21** payoff): every interception correlates with Nine-brokered jobs. Only Nine's.

**A3-S11 — "The Rat."**
Confrontation at Nine's forge-shop, played sorrowful, not shouting: Adam lays out the correlation like a proof. Nine folds — and the mechanism is worse than betrayal: **his sister**, in state custody, her safety a dial Marr turns (double-agent reveal; his prosperity ticks, C-20, retro-flagged). "You'd have done the same." "Maybe. You'll do different now." Adam flips him — triple agent — and the first counterfeed is immediate: a false safehouse location sold upward. Aegis burns an empty barge at dawn. The barge was Kray's — *volunteered* — and the sight of Aegis torching her property does what no cause could: **the Ferrywoman is in** (wildcard turned; her price, stated flatly: "the Vats toll. Someone's finally going to pay it."). One clean week begins — the first silence in Adam's head since the raid.

**A3-S12 — "Canary Trap." — REVEAL R3**
With Nine controlled, exactly one leak remains: the channel Adam never audited because it only ever *gave*. He runs a canary trap — three meets, three channels; ROOK9 alone gets the church option. Aegis hits the church. The proof sits on the board like a checkmate delivered by mail, and then the corpus analysis lands the name the player has been invited (C-05, C-06, C-10, C-11, C-14, C-29) to dread: engineering idiom; allocution dates matching one supervision window; a warrant that knew the notebooks; forged math using a taught shortcut; *castle early, castle often* — an opening habit reproduced across nineteen ROOK9 games. **Rook is Ray Cordell.** (**R3**.) Flashback stitch — A0-S05's expensive nothing replayed with sound on. Adam does not rage. He goes very, very quiet, and asks the Verger for one clean suit.

**A3-S13 — "One Last Game."**
Cordell's lake cabin. The scene the whole script protects: Adam arrives as the student — no accusation, no wire, one chessboard. Two old men's rituals performed perfectly by two liars: whiskey poured, weather discussed, a game played. Cordell — warm, funny, *paternal*, and (the audience now knows) the architect — offers, unprompted, escape money and a door: "If you ever stop running, there's a way out of the city that doesn't end in a courtroom. Old men keep doors." (The same door. His door. **R4 pre-echo.**) Adam declines with a smile, and — the scene's only weapon — **castles late**, breaking the habit Cordell installed. Says nothing. Leaves. Cordell sits alone with the board and reads the move for what it is; his hand is not quite steady clearing the pieces. Both men now know, and neither has said a word: the hunt is finally honest. From this beat forward, **Adam is ahead for the first time** (the antagonist-advantage inversion completes: Nine flipped, Rook burned, choreography exposed).

**A3-S14 — "Lena."**
Fallout salvage: one true detail in Duval's burned story — the word *nightingale* — matched a phrase in a 1974 letter **Lena Voss** found in her dead grandfather's papers (**C-30**). She reaches out through Duval (the failed story functioning, structurally, as the beacon that finds the last ally: every defeat in this act seeds its own answer). At the Lantern: forensic hydrologist, Voss face, Lowmarsh anger, the watch-fob key's lock (a family archive strongbox) — letters, 1968–1975, in Aldous-line hands, discussing the Covenant in the domestic plural. Clean provenance the frame never mapped. Her terms: "The name confesses. It doesn't settle."

**A3-S15 — "Mara's Ledger."**
The pin answered (a chalked clinic symbol at the cemetery gate — her channel, her tradecraft, learned alone). One meeting, a night ward, whispering between cribs: Mara has rebuilt the cluster map from twenty years of triage ledgers — onset dates, addresses, the 2014 inflection (**C-01** payoff; independently corroborating the institute's secret study). She has also, quietly, verified the Violet Marsh phone predates its own "purchase" (her own frame-crack, found unprompted — she never needed him to be innocent on faith; she needed data; she is, after all, the person he married). No reconciliation — withheld deliberately: "This isn't for you. It's for them." And, at the door, not turning: "The signature on that account was traced from our mortgage. I sign my E backwards. They didn't know that. You did." *She knows.* (RH-03 fully debunked — for the player, before Adam dares believe it.)

**A3-S16 — "The Plan." — ACT TURN**
Everything on the board; the board is insufficient — that is the plan's premise. Documentary war is lost *by design* (R2, A3-S07): the Vestry owns every well the truth could be poured into. The Verger says the operative line over the wreckage of the paper strategy: **"Paper burns. Water remembers."** Inversion (R2.5, strategic): stop testifying. **Demonstrate.** The vote is in nine days, inside a broadcast ritual the deal itself invented (first-draw certification sampling, C-03's ceremony clause); Storm **Vesper** is inbound off the coast, upgraded twice; the Nightingale system is a machine, and machines have load limits, and Adam Vale is the best water engineer this city ever trained. Heist-movie planning grammar, roles assigned (A4-S01). Final image of the act: Adam at the drowned chapel's edge, notebook #42 open to a fresh page, writing the plan's first line — *Make the water testify.* **End of Act III.**

## ACT IV — RECKONING
*Judgment: the name cleared publicly, revenge in the measure the player chooses, the machine stopped. Tone: convergent — every act's debts called in. The storm and the vote arrive together, because the Vestry scheduled the vote and God scheduled the storm, and only one of them can be lobbied.*

**A4-S01 — "Nine Days."**
Prep-mission suite (playable, interleaved):
- **The Sampler:** Whitlock's file (H.G. + Fidelis + obstruction paper) walks into the State Attorney General's office at last — producing not an arrest but the needed inch: **Dr. Amara Chen**, State Water Board, will personally conduct the ceremony's first-draw certification sampling, with sealed duplicates. Kessler cannot object to compliance theater without breaking her own script (her ritual, hijacked — the plan's central irony).
- **The Route:** Kray runs the Vats approach — the toll finally paid in the coin she always meant: witness. 
- **The Ledger:** the Verger surfaces the Concordance from its forty-year hide (a coffin-shaped waterproof case, because of course it is).
- **The Letters:** Lena and Duval authenticate the family archive — and Duval preps the *real* story under a vow that defines her arc: **it runs second.** Truth first, byline after.
- **The Lie:** Nine's last forgery — for the good guys this time: the credentials that put Adam's people inside the ceremony's service perimeter.

**A4-S02 — "The Concordance." — REVEAL R4**
The drowned Vestry crypt beneath St. Alban's — the club's first hall, flooded since 1968 — the game's final riddle gauntlet: hymn-cipher, ledger-stones, the watch-fob key's twin lock (Voss's key + the Verger's memory — the two exiles' halves united, thematically load-bearing). Inside: the **Concordance** — cipher-minutes 1877–1974, the Quiet Covenant vote itself... and appended in a younger hand the Verger never had the heart to show anyone: the routing copy of **Cordell's 1988 certification**, annotated by the Quorum's then-secretary: *"The young man signed. He will be of use for thirty years."* (**R4** — the First Lie, and the full answer to *why Adam*: TS-19/44 would have unspooled to 1988; the mentor authored the student's erasure to keep his own from surfacing. **"You weren't framed for what you'd do. You were framed for what he did."**) Also here, filed with the Covenant's operational annexes: the archive-heist choreography memo (**C-17** payoff — the staged box, the exit-alarm, the compromise order pre-drafted *before* the break-in). The player's proudest win, A2-S07, re-graded before their eyes into the enemy's move. The room is silent except for water.

**A4-S03 — "Marr's File." — R5 ARMED**
Nine's triple-cross delivers his masterpiece: from Aegis's own contingency vault, the Vestry's succession-and-containment dossier — post-vote cleanup protocols, and the designated author of all crimes should the arrangement ever surface: **MARR, S.** — the fixer himself, pre-framed by his own employers, evidence pre-staged, a Skerry-style witness pre-retained (**C-31**; the playbook's next edition, already typeset). Adam reads it twice and does the coldest, cleanest thing in the game: mails it to Marr. No note. No ask. Just the ledger, shown to the man whose name is newly in it. (Chekhov's fixer, cocked.)

**A4-S04 — "Eve of Vesper."**
The quiet-before scene, earned: the Lantern, all of them, soup and lamplight, the Verger's benediction v2 ("For the drowned and the discarded — stand up"). And one honest conversation, Adam and Mara, on the flooded chapel steps — no reconciliation offered or asked: "Come back out of this one alive, and we'll see who you are then. I'd like to meet him." Rain begins. It does not stop for three scenes.

**A4-S05 — "Ratification Day." — FINALE BEGINS**
Ascension Plaza, the renovated Waterworks Cathedral (the old pump station made a secular temple — the Vestry's aesthetic self-portrait, purchased with public money). Livestreamed vote; Deane beaming; Hale on the dais rereading his own doubts; Kessler ascendant in grey. Vesper arrives on schedule — *their* schedule assumed rain; nobody's assumed **this**: the city's hundred-year storm surging the river as the cameras go live. Cross-cut deployment: Whitlock and Dr. Chen at the sampling pavilion, credentials immaculate (Nine's best work); Kray's boats staged in the storm tunnels; the Verger with Duval, the Concordance and letters sealed and *withheld* (nothing publishes until the water speaks — discipline as doctrine now); Adam and Lena descending to the Nightingale hall through water already violet at the seams.

**A4-S06 — "The Nightingale Falls." — SETPIECE + R5 PAYOFF**
The pump hall at storm load, screaming. Adam's masterstroke is an engineer's, not a saboteur's (design thesis: his superpower, fully expressed): he does not destroy the Nightingale — he **re-routes it**, valve by valve, a final full-grammar hydraulics puzzle under time and floodwater — sending seventy years of intercepted leachate *to daylight*: the storm drains, the plaza's showcase fountain loop, the ceremonial first-draw main. The storm supplies the violence; Adam only opens the doors. Aegis's crew arrives — and behind them, **Marr**, file in hand, and the standoff the whole game has priced: Marr looks at the machine, the rising violet water, his own name in the Vestry's ledger — and (canonical) gives the only order left that serves *order*: **"Contract's void. Fall back."** Aegis walks. The fixer takes his insurance archive and leaves his employers naked to the weather (player-choice branch: refuse the standoff and fight him — the hall's flood mechanics make it the game's hardest encounter; canonically he walks, and it lands colder). Lena, at the junction wheel, gets the act's best quiet line: "Grandfather's money dug this. I'm just returning it."

**A4-S07 — "Purple Rain." — THE SCENE**
Above, live, in one unbroken broadcast: the vote passes — applause — Deane raises the ceremonial first-draw glass as Vesper peaks — and the glass fills **faint violet**. A beat of pure broadcast silence. Then the plaza fountain erupts lavender; the storm drains backing up through Lowmarsh send the streets' floodwater running *purple downhill into the plaza*, sheeting around the stage; Dr. Chen — on camera, by the deal's own compliance script — draws, seals, and states for the record; a million phones film the sky and streets of Meridian raining violet over the Ascension. The folklore, the graffiti, the window, the title: paid off in a single image. Duval's story drops *second*, as sworn: the Concordance, the letters, the clinic map, TS-19/44 re-validated against live chemistry. **You cannot indict the rain.** Kessler does not run. She straightens her jacket, watches the water find its level, and starts making calls — which is scarier.

**A4-S08 — "The Undercroft Door."**
Cordell is not on the dais and was never going to be. Adam knows where he is because he knows him: down, at the beginning — the 1988 seal chamber by the old outfall, water rising past its cornerstone. Final confrontation, no boss bar: an old man, a chessboard set on a survey crate, and the game's whole argument in dialogue. Cordell's confession is self-justification with perfect engineering: one signature, then forty years of "maintenance"; the city's every honest pipe as amortization on a single lie. He offers Adam the last door — a prepared exit, money, silence, *"you of all people know the record can say anything"* — the temptation stated in the hero's own stolen creed. Player choice, tracked, canonical third: **let the water take him / leave him to the flood he built / drag him out for trial.** Canonical: Adam hauls his mentor up through the rising violet dark and delivers him to Whitlock at the waterline with the game's title-fight line: **"You are what you sign. Sign this."** (The arrest report. He does.)

**A4-S09 — "Fallout."**
Days, compressed, documentary-style: emergency injunction voids the transfer pre-closing; the State AG and federal validators descend; **Hale flips in seventy-two hours** flat — C-27, the impossible wire, gives him the door his self-preservation was scratching at ("I was fed a case. I can prove it, because I kept what they fed me") — his proffer buries Kessler and ends his own career as a footnote; **Kessler is arrested at the airfield**, composed, lawyered, and convicted in the end not by any single document but by the **lattice** — Concordance + letters + clinic ledger + escrow trail + live sampling + Marr's archive, arriving by courier, unsigned — every strand from a source the frame never thought to poison, because every strand came from someone the Vestry had already discarded (the theme, stated as a prosecution exhibit list). Charges against Adam: dismissed with prejudice; Skerry's posthumous "insurance" tapes of his own coaching sessions (preserved, of course, in Marr's archive) and the Grady anachronism formally vacate the record-poisoning — TS-19/44 is re-certified, *his stamp restored by the same statute that voided it*. The exoneration press conference is held on the courthouse steps in light rain. **Adam does not attend.** He is at Theo's grave, reading the charges-dismissed order aloud, once, quietly, and leaving it under a stone — paper, finally, doing the only job it was ever good for: witness, not weapon.

**A4-S10 — "What Heaven Is Now." — EPILOGUE**
Months. The consent decree; remediation cranes over the Vats — the contractors seized from Kessler's trust, operating under Lena's re-chartered **Voss Remediation Fund** (the dye money paying its bill at last). The Lantern, legal now, still run on three rules; the Verger declines his reinstatement — "I keep the door" — but is seen, once, sweeping St. Alban's drained crypt like a man who might someday hold a service. Kray's boats carry inspectors and schoolchildren, licensed, exorbitant. Duval's second story wins the prize her first one should have; she teaches now — sourcing, mostly. Whitlock runs the new public-integrity unit and remains professionally unimpressed by everyone. Nine, sentence suspended for cooperation, makes only legal documents and is bored beyond description; his sister walks free. And Adam — reinstated, older, new house on the *dry* side of nothing, because nowhere in Meridian is far from water and he wouldn't want it to be — meets Mara for coffee on a Saturday. It is not the old life. It is a first date, ten years and one apocalypse after the wedding. She laughs at something. The rain outside is ordinary, and stays that way. Final beat, the creed closed: at the MWA field office, Adam hands a terrified new engineer a blank field notebook. *"The data doesn't lie."* Beat. *"People do. Write it down anyway."* — Fade on Meridian in clean rain, the sky rinsed of everything but weather.

**A4-S11 — (Optional post-credit sting; sequel/DLC hook, non-canonical-load-bearing.)**
The Vestry's correspondence room, sealed by court order. On the evidence table, among the ciphered letter-books: outbound correspondence, decades of it, addressed to vestries, guilds, and "improvement societies" in eleven other cities. One envelope, recent, outgoing, unsent. The stamp is unfamiliar. The rain, somewhere else, is presumably still ordinary. For now.

# PART FIVE — THEMES, TONE, AND TITLE

## 6. Themes

1. **Who writes the ledger.** Records are not truth; they are *authored*. Every institution in the game — court, archive, church, press, chess app — is a ledger with an author, and power is the ability to hold the pen. The hero's journey is from *trusting* the record, to *fighting* the record, to *stepping outside* the record entirely (the water), to — the resolution — taking up the pen honestly. The final creed amendment ("write it down *anyway*") is the theme's answer: records are corruptible and we must keep them anyway, because the alternative is the Vestry.
2. **What the city discards.** The Vestry's fatal blind spot is structural, not tactical: it cannot model people it has written off — the exile, the crank, the smuggler, the junkie's brother, the fugitive's wife, the poor parish. Every instrument of Adam's victory comes from the discard pile. Hell is survivable because hell is *populated*, and the discarded owe the ledger nothing.
3. **You are what you sign.** Integrity as identity, literalized: stamps, allocutions, confessions, forged initials, a backwards E. Cordell is the thesis's dark proof — one signature, metastasized into a life. Adam is its counter-proof.
4. **Revenge vs. restoration.** The game gives revenge honestly (Krebs, Marr, Cordell — each a player valve) and prices it honestly. The canonical path's argument: the best revenge on people who author reality is to *make reality unauthorable* — then let the ordinary machinery of consequence, which they corrupted, work honestly on them. Adam's revenge is jurisdictional: he returns his enemies to the systems they owned, after breaking their ownership.
5. **Paper burns; water remembers.** The elemental thesis. The conspiracy is a war of documents against a river, and the river was always going to win; Adam's genius is only ever *scheduling* the victory. Heaven and hell, in the end, are both water: the flood that takes everything, the rain that rinses it clean.

## 7. Tone

Grounded neo-noir thriller with mythic undertones — the heaven/hell arc is figurative, carried by architecture (Heights/Flats/Undercroft), weather, and liturgical residue (the Vestry's church-flavored menace), never by fantasy. Rain-soaked violet-and-sodium palette; melancholy but propulsive. Violence is scarce, physical, and priced. Humor lives in the Undercroft (Nine, Kray) because gallows humor is how the discarded stay people. The conspiracy is never operatic: its horror is *administrative* — clipboards in the pump hall, a $3.1M landscaping line, a plea deal's date range. Reference points per GAME_DESIGN.md stand (The Fugitive, Monte Cristo, Dishonored, Deus Ex, Inferno), with one addition: *Chinatown* — for water, land, and the price of finding out.

## 8. The Title

Five nested meanings, revealed in order: (1) folklore — Lowmarsh's omen, *when the rain runs purple, the Works are leaking*; (2) history — mauveine, the purple dye that built and poisoned the city; (3) the frame — "Violet Marsh," the conspiracy signing its work in purple; (4) the climax — the storm-day daylighting, the sky and streets testifying in violet on live broadcast; (5) the elegy — rain as grief and absolution, the thing that falls on the just and unjust and, eventually, rinses both. (Resolves GAME_DESIGN.md's open question on the title.)
